Highlights

  • Deep learning can be used for direct spatial-temporal mapping of water ponds.
  • Class imbalance and label uncertainty are key factors during model training.
  • Ensemble models produced more robust results.
  • Different water ponding timing and intensity behaviors were found across areas.
  • Measuring changes in pond connectivity increases the knowledge of runoff formation.

Abstract

Rainfall simulations are an established method to gain knowledge on small-scale hydrological processes like infiltration, ponding and surface runoff. Due to limitations in measuring methods, these processes are often assumed to happen homogeneously, even though they vary on a subplot scale.

Within this study, we took high-resolution time-lapse images of several plots to observe and quantify ponding and the formation of connectivity and surface runoff. We investigated the potential of deep learning to segment water ponding areas in time-lapse images during rainfall simulations and to estimate ponding time.

We trained three different convolutional neural networks, considering classification uncertainty and imbalance of the ground-truth data, as well as ensemble modeling and spatial correlation between samples. Addressing annotation uncertainty and imbalance was more important here than the choice of network itself, and ensemble models improved robustness.
